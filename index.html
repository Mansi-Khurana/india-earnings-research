Introduction

Why Education Matters for Earnings

Education is widely regarded as one of the most important determinants of earnings. According to Human Capital Theory, individuals invest in education to acquire skills and knowledge that increase their productivity and earning potential — and employers reward this with higher wages.

This study tests that hypothesis empirically using India's PLFS 2017–18 dataset, isolating the effect of education while controlling for age and gender.

Descriptive Evidence

Earnings Rise Steadily with Education

A clear upward pattern emerges when average monthly earnings are plotted across educational levels — from ₹7,258 for non-literate workers all the way to ₹29,774 for postgraduates.

Key Findings

What the Data Tells Us

📈
Education pays — consistently

Each additional year of formal education is associated with ₹1,474 more in monthly earnings, even after controlling for age and gender. The effect is robust across all three model specifications.

🎓
4× earnings gap between non-literate and postgraduate

Workers with no literacy earn ₹7,258/month on average. Postgraduates earn ₹29,774 — a 310% premium, underlining the immense labour market value of higher education in India.

👤
Age proxies experience and rewards it

After including age as a proxy for work experience in Model 2, the education coefficient actually increases from ₹1,340 to ₹1,483, suggesting age partially confounded the raw estimate.

⚠️
Gender gap persists even after controlling for education

Female workers earn ₹3,333 less per month than male workers with the same education and age, indicating structural wage disparities beyond human capital differences.

Methodology

How the Analysis Was Built

The study follows a sequential OLS regression approach, progressively adding control variables to decompose the effect of education on earnings and test for omitted variable bias.

01
Data Filtering
Filtered 433K PLFS observations to 42,794 regular salaried workers using STATA's keep if command.
02
Descriptive Analysis
Computed average earnings by education category using tabstat to identify raw patterns.
03
OLS Regression
Estimated three models using regress, adding age then gender sequentially to assess omitted variable bias.
04
Model Comparison
Compared coefficients and R-squared across models to evaluate explanatory power and robustness.
Limitations

What This Study Cannot Claim

  • Cross-sectional data from PLFS 2017–18 allows correlation, not causal inference. Unobservables like ability, ambition, and family background may drive both education and earnings.
  • Sample is restricted to regular salaried workers — findings cannot be generalised to self-employed, informal, or gig workers who form a large share of India's labour market.
  • Age is used as a proxy for work experience, which may not perfectly capture actual labour market tenure or on-the-job skill acquisition.
  • Occupation, industry, field of study, quality of institution, and urban/rural location are omitted, potentially biasing the education coefficient.
